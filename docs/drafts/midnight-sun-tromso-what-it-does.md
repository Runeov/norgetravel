<p class="text-lg text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">At 00:40 in late June I walked up the Sherpatrappa stairs above Tromsø with a coffee and no headlamp. The sun sat low over Kvaløya, the harbour cranes threw long shadows across the water, and a group of visitors stood at the top arguing about why their northern lights tour had been cancelled. It had not been cancelled. There are no northern lights in a Tromsø summer. The sky was the same pale gold it had been at noon, and it would stay that way until I gave up and went to bed three hours later. That gap between what people expect and what the sky delivers is the whole story of a summer trip to Tromsø.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-[#1A365D] mt-10 mb-4">What the midnight sun is, and the dates that matter</h2>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Tromsø sits at 69.6 degrees north, roughly 350 km above the Arctic Circle. At that latitude the sun does not dip below the horizon for about ten weeks each summer. In 2026 the midnight sun in Tromsø runs from roughly 18 May to 26 July. During that window the sun stays up 24 hours a day. It circles the sky and drops toward the northern horizon around midnight, but it never sets. There is no sunrise and no sunset, because the sun never leaves.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">The mirror image is mørketid (the polar night), the weeks when the sun never climbs above the horizon at all. In Tromsø the polar night runs from about 27 November to 15 January. The mountains around the city extend the dark in practice, so functional daylight thins out from roughly 21 November to 21 January. One number frames the whole year: at 69.6 degrees north you get about 76 days of continuous daylight in summer and about 49 days of continuous night in winter. Tromsø does not have a normal day. It has two opposite extremes and a short, fast transition between them.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-[#1A365D] mt-10 mb-4">The aurora reality check</h2>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Read this part if you booked a summer trip to see the northern lights. You will not see them. The aurora is always there in the upper atmosphere, but you can only see it against a dark sky. From mid-May to late July the sky never goes dark in Tromsø, so the aurora is invisible. It is not weak or hidden. It is washed out by 24-hour daylight.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">The aurora season in Tromsø runs from October to March. Those are the months with enough darkness to see the lights, with November to February giving the longest nights and the best odds. If northern lights are the reason you are coming, book between October and March, give yourself three nights minimum, and accept that two of them might be cloudy. A summer trip cannot deliver the aurora at any price. The tour operators who sell "northern lights" packages in June are selling you a clear sky and a bus ride. Save your money and chase the lights in the dark season instead.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-[#1A365D] mt-10 mb-4">What 24-hour light actually does</h2>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Three things change when the sun stops setting. Your body clock loses its anchor. Daylight tells your brain to stay awake, so without darkness your melatonin never gets the signal that the day is over. Most visitors report poor sleep for the first two or three nights. You will feel wired at 01:00 and groggy at 09:00. Locals manage it with discipline and blackout blinds, not willpower.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Your photos change too. The flat overhead light of midday Tromsø in July is harsh and gives you blown-out skies and dull water. The "night" photos people expect, dark sky with a glow, do not exist here in summer. What you get instead is a long golden window. From about 21:00 to 01:00 the sun sits low in the north and the light turns warm and raking, which is the best photographic light of the day. Plan to shoot late, not at noon.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">And the crowds change. Cruise ships and tour buses move on Tromsø in the middle of the day. Viewpoints like Fjellheisen and the Sherpatrappa stairs are busy from 11:00 to 18:00 and empty after 22:00. The light that ruins your sleep is the same light that hands you the city at midnight with nobody in it.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-[#1A365D] mt-10 mb-4">How to use it instead of fighting it</h2>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Hike late to dodge the crowds. The Fjellheisen cable car runs to the Storsteinen viewpoint at 421 m, reopens for the season on 1 June, and runs late into the night through the midnight sun period, far later than a normal viewpoint closes. Ride up at 22:00 or walk the Sherpatrappa stone staircase, and you get the harbour, the Tromsdalen valley, and the open sea in low gold light with a fraction of the daytime queue. The same logic works for any trail near the city. Start your walk at 20:00, not 10:00.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Protect your sleep on purpose. Bring a proper sleep mask, and confirm your accommodation has blackout blinds before you book, because many older Tromsø flats do not. Pick a consistent bedtime and hold it even though the sun is still up. Treat the midnight light as a feature to use for a few late evenings, not as permission to stop sleeping. The travellers who burn out by day three are the ones who tried to stay up "because it was light."</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Time your photos for the golden window. Shoot landscapes and the city between 21:00 and 01:00 when the sun is low. Skip the noon shots. If you want one anchor activity built entirely around the light, the Midnight Sun Marathon runs in Tromsø on 20 June 2026, with the full 42 km marathon, half marathon, 10 km, and shorter distances staged so runners finish under the low midnight sun. It is the northernmost AIMS-certified marathon in the world, and registration fills up, so book early if you want it.</p>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-[#1A365D] mt-10 mb-4">Who this helps, and who should reconsider summer</h2>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Come to Tromsø in summer if you want long, warm hikes without a headlamp, late kayak trips, midnight viewpoints with no crowds, and a running of the Midnight Sun Marathon. The 24-hour light is a real tool for anyone who plans around it. You can pack more daylight activity into a day here than anywhere else in Europe.</p>

<p class="text-slate-700 leading-relaxed">Reconsider summer if the northern lights are your goal. They do not appear under the midnight sun, and no operator can change that. Move that trip to the October to March window and book three nights to give the weather a chance. Reconsider summer too if you sleep badly in bright rooms and cannot secure blackout blinds, because three nights of broken sleep will cost you the trip. Match the season to what you actually want, and Tromsø rewards you. Get it wrong, and you will stand on a viewpoint at midnight wondering where the lights are.</p>
